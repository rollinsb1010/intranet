The Role of Scientific Research in SEDL’s Work

Group Notes for SECAC Sessions II and III: August 12-15, 2002

SECAC met during its retreat, August 12-15, to discuss the role of scientific research in SEDL’s work and to answer the questions posed in sessions II and III. Subgroups addressed the questions first and the whole-group discussion followed. We agree with many of the points made in the synthesized report. Following are comments SECAC would add:

Session II

Question 1: Are the six principles given in the NRC report adequate for defining scientific research in education? If not, how do you think they need to be modified?

In general SECAC supports the principles of scientific research. A primary concern is how SECAC could better assist its clients in the application of scientific research practices, strategies, etc., in the context of the six principles.

Question 2: Given your group’s definition, how do you think SEDL should attend to scientific research and the quality (rigor) of it? What would we need to do differently to act on scientific research in the manner you recommend?

As a group, we need to learn what scientifically based research means and how to apply it to our work. Use future staff meetings to discuss strategies on using the principles in our work. Learn how to use the principles when assisting clients in a way that does not make us “talking heads.” Make presentations in a language that the client will understand. Create “process questions” to use when working with clients.

Question 3: To be a successful RD&D institution, where success is defined in terms of our ability to build knowledge and bring it to bear on practice, are there other types of knowledge that we need to pay attention to beyond that which comes from scientific research? If so, what are they, how well do we attend to them, and what would we do in order to better act on them?

Other types of knowledge we need are:

  • Marketing theory
  • Change theory
  • Adult learning
  • School culture/anthropology

We need to be able to:

  • Help teachers to move scientific knowledge into practical application
  • Integrate lots of areas when working with schools. Use the team approach to maximize staff strengths.

Session III

Again, SECAC engaged in this session at its retreat. SECAC used a different set of questions for this discussion. The primary reason was that in our role of working in conjunction with the U.S. Department of Education, we believe we will have a major responsibility in assisting schools and school districts in implementing many USDE programs, e.g., Reading First. Therefore, our discussion centered on understanding the National Reading Panel (NRP) report and its structure and limitations as well as its implications in our work. Following are answers to the questions subsumed from our discussion.

Question 1: What did you learn from this session’s readings that changed your understanding of scientific research in education?

There is a gap that separates researchers from practitioners. All research involves a trust factor.

Question 2: Did this particular case lead you to change your thoughts about any of the principles in defining scientific research you felt were important from your position in Session II? If so, how?

This particular case seems to be narrow in its focus, particularly because of the type of research it is—a meta-analysis. There are also many other types of research that are scientific that the NRP did not consider when establishing its criteria for inclusion in the analysis.

Question 3: Would you change your responses to any of the other questions you addressed in the previous session concerning SEDL’s attention to scientific research and other sources of knowledge based on your discussion in this session? If so, what changes would you make?

This question was not addressed in our discussion.

Question 4: Does the Session II synthesis accurately reflect your group’s discussion?

For the most part it does. Because SECAC addressed Session II after the synthesis was posted, we had the opportunity to compare our responses to the other responses from Session II. Points of departure were noted.

Question 5: Do you want to continue the dialogue on scientifically based research via a listserv or a bulletin board?

SECAC recognized the need to continue in face-to-face discussions. Because we are geographically distant from SEDL, comparing what we think as a unit to what other integrated groups think is important, but we would not replace the face-to-face discussions.

Additional notes from the discussion:

The NRP report is identified as scientific research, but we recognize that this research is inherently value-laden. To accept it as scientific research, you have to buy into the criteria that the NRP established to include a study in its meta-analysis. If the criteria are accepted, then the subsequent analysis and conclusions make sense. If the criteria are viewed as too narrow a focus, or dismissed, then there are lots of questions about the report.
