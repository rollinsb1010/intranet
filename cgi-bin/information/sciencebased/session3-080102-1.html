Group 1

Group members present: Jennifer Beck, Stella Bell, Leslie Blair (recorder), Deborah Donnelly, Marilyn Heath, Ted Hull, Marsha Lloyd, Stephen Marble (reporter), Elaine Phillips, Mary Wolcott (facilitator)

Question 1: What did you learn from this session’s readings that changed your understandings of scientific research in education?

Group 1 discussed the need to consider context in research—methodology alone can’t negate context. There was discussion on the “messiness” of research, including the difficulty of adhering to the six principles when you are in the field and conducting research with ordinary people. The principles in the book are based on a “perfect world,” commented one group member. The National Reading Panel (NRP) study is an example of research in the real world and this panel was subject to political pressures and time pressures.

Question 2: Did this particular case lead you to change your thoughts about any of the principles defining scientific research you felt were important from your discussion in Session II? If so, how?

Our group decided that more value needed to be placed on the design principles in the book Scientific Research in Education. These include the need to create structures to guide agendas and monitor work; insulate agency from political interference; and develop a balanced portfolio of short-, medium-, and long-term issues of importance to policy and practice.

Question 3: Would you change your responses to any of the other questions you addressed in the previous session concerning SEDL’s attention to scientific research and other sources of knowledge based on your discussions in this session? If so what changes would you make?

Group 1 still sees the need to come to a working definition of scientifically based research. The group discussed the need to operationalize the six scientific principles and how the definition might vary in different arenas—at SEDL, for higher education, for practitioners.

Question 4: Does the Session II synthesis accurately reflect your group’s discussion?

Under Question 2, the second bullet, we thought there had been more discussion on being proactive about becoming a more theoretically based RD&D institution, not just being more proactive in securing funding.

Question 5: Do you want to continue the dialogue on scientifically based research via a listserv or a bulletin board?

Most of Group 1 members were not enthusiastic about a bulletin board or a listserv. Most felt that for such electronic conversations to work effectively, participants would need to be highly motivated with the time to engage in such discussion.

Additional notes from the discussion:

Our discussion of the NRP study focused on the political agenda that seemed to be in place. One group member felt “in this case, the political agenda covers itself in a scientific mantle.”

There was discussion on Russell Whitehurst’s view of what constitutes scientifically based research and whether that is the view that SEDL as an institution should take. There was also quite a bit of discussion on SEDL and other similar institutions being “beholden” to the federal government for our funding and how this affects the research that we carry out. The group concluded that by trying to be more “politically correct” to satisfy the feds’ current definition of research, we would actually be moving away from the six scientific principles that everyone had generally agreed were sound. There was further discussion that the scientific principles were a powerful lens through which to view research.

Because so much of the SEDL staff spends time in the field with practitioners, the group also discussed our role ofproviding “scientifically based research” to practitioners. Group members mentioned that many school and district staff are totally focused on NCLB and they want help with regard to what is scientifically based research. If we used the narrow definition of scientifically based research, we would have little to offer, when in reality there is much research if we use a broader definition. There was discussion about being more expansive with research findings, e.g., identifying them as good descriptive studies and being more specific about the role the findings play in the research scheme of things.

The group then turned its attention to our role as creators of research and information instead of as disseminators. There was some discussion of work produced in the field not being valued in the institution; the point was made that even though SEDL staff may be doing training, there was a lot of background research-related work that was necessary to get to that point. Also, our work with practitioners enables us to add to the knowledge base.

One group member observed that because our research was not experimental, it seemed we were being pushed out of the research arena into the technical assistance arena. This led us back to the discussion we had during Session II on July 22 about being proactive in becoming an independent research organization that can be competitive for the knowledge-building funding. We see our strength as being contextual, we are working in schools most of the time, but how do we build our research presence when 75 percent of our staff members are more practitioners than researchers?

There was also some discussion on how fluid the organization is in terms of staffing and what that means to a research organization that is theory driven. The group also noted our need to connect to the current research community and participate in it. [ UP ]

Group 2

Group members present: Sean Claes, Johanna Franke (recorder), Ashley Galaway, Margarita Greer (facilitator), Cori Groth, Wes Hoover, Kim Hughes, Patricia Keith, Vangie Orozco, Joe Parker (reporter), Sue Street (synthesizer)

Question 1: What did you learn from this session’s readings that changed your understanding of scientific research in education?

Legitimizing only scientifically based research has the potential to eliminate some of the more complex phenomena.

  • You’re not asking some of the most important questions because they’re not easily measured.
  • Is “scientifically based research” a code term for strictly experimental research? No, the six principles say the method needs to match the question, but the method does not have to be experimental. Some areas of study lend themselves to more experimental/quasi-experimental methodologies; those areas that do not are not being explored.

Group 2 members say they still aren’t really clear on what scientific research in education means after reading the assigned articles for today’s session. They also believe SEDL should revisit the six principles and determine exactly what they mean to the institution as a whole; otherwise, different people will interpret them in different ways.

Question 2: Did this particular case lead you to change your thoughts about any of the principles defining scientific research you felt were important from your discussion in Session II? If so, how?

  • SEDL may need to explore how methods such as the descriptive method fit into a scientifically based research definition. Descriptive data and empirical data should inform one another.
  • The six principles should direct researchers to explicitly describe assumptions/underpinnings of theory in their research.
  • More clarification of what the principles mean would help SEDL staff talk about scientifically based research in the field.

Question 3: Would you change your responses to any of the other questions you addressed in the previous session concerning SEDL’s attention to scientific research and other sources of knowledge based on your discussions in this session? If so, what changes would you make?

We’d add the following to the answer to Question 2, “What does SEDL need to do in order to ensure high-quality work based on the definition of scientifically based research?”

  • Develop an understanding and possible uses of what SEDL has already done in related work. Build on previous project learnings and gather the information using a specific type of methodology. Do this with a database complete with knowledge management that can ensure data are useful to people viewing the database in the future.
  • Examine SEDL’s quality assurance process to see how it aligns with the six principles and emphasize peer review.

We’d also add the following to the answer to Question 3, “Are there other types of knowledge beyond scientific research that bears on practice? If so, what are they, do we attend to them, and how can we better act on them?”

  • Some education questions may have answers based on research done outside the educational field, e.g., anthropology, sociology, psychology, political science, and economics.

Question 4: Does the Session II synthesis accurately reflect your group’s discussion?

We should be more explicit about “our work” in the first bullet of Question 2 (“SEDL should conduct institution-wide discussions to strengthen our collective understanding of the theoretical bases for our work.”) by listing the six focus areas that make up SEDL’s work:

  • Improving school performance
  • Strengthening teaching and learning in content areas
  • Integrating technology into teaching and learning
  • Involving family and community in student learning
  • Connecting disability research to practice
  • Supporting policy development through research

Question 5: Do you want to continue the dialogue on scientifically based research via a listserv or a bulletin board?

  • If it is a bulletin board, it needs to be facilitated and synthesized on a regular basis and shared with everyone. An e-mail summarizing the bulletin board activity should be sent out once a week.
  • It should expand, not eliminate, the face-to-face cross-lab meetings. It needs to include all of SEDL, including the Dana Center.

Additional notes from the discussion:

  • The reporting in the articles and NRP report did not appear to indicate the use of the latest research methods.
  • The NRP limited their methodology because of their narrow charge.
  • The NRP report provides research components, but it also lists limitation after limitation for each component. You can’t conclude anything. So how do we advise people in the field?
  • It is difficult to judge from the articles if the questions asked and answered match the methods being used.
  • Meta-analysis may not be all it is supposed to be (“The term ‘garbage-in, garbage-out’ underscores the inability of a broad statistical analysis to exceed the caliber of the studies it uses,” p. 206 of Gerald Coles’s “Reading Taught to the Tune of the ‘Scientific’ Hickory Stick.”). [ UP ]

Group 3

Group members present: Mary Ann Brittan, Joan Buttram (facilitator), Jerry Elder, Cheryl Harris (recorder), Brian Litke (reporter), Jill Slack, John Westbrook (synthesizer)

Question 1: What did you learn from this session’s readings that changed your understanding of scientific research in education?

The group discussed two central issues that the readings brought to the forefront:

  • The potential for bias to distort findings, e.g., theoretical camps may not feel they are biased.
    • The issue of bias behind scientific efforts—just because something has “national” in its title or is a review of literature does not mean that it is good or accurate science.
    • Variability in perspectives influences what research is done and the way it is done.
  • Importance of professional critique and scrutiny.
    • Critique of the National Reading Panel (NRP) report—what was its theoretical base? There was little explicit chain of reasoning or a coherent explanation of the theory it was based upon. They did not do such a good job of disclosing the research for review or critique before it was published.
    • Professional scrutiny is good—if this is one of the “best case” examples of scientific research in education, what does that say for the other fields of education?

Question 2: Did this particular case lead you to change your thoughts about any of the principles defining scientific research you felt were important from your discussion in Session II? If so, how?

After a brief discussion about the methodological overview in the NRP report (p. 5), we proceeded to examine the NRP report as it related to the six principles of scientific research:

  • Is it fair to apply these six principles to a synthesis of research?
    • Yes, though there was some dissention about whether that was an appropriate objective for a synthesis of literature. We decided this is an example of a poor job of doing scientific research, and that the principles themselves were not defective. The principles were not completely followed by the NRP report committee members.
  • Would we modify the six principles of doing scientific research in any way?
    • Yes. We thought about the “ethics” principle that we discussed in Session II and decided that it may be worthwhile to have a seventh principle that explicitly states the importance of stating your theoretical perspectives, the context (e.g., political influences), limitations, mediating factors, etc.
  • Questions to ask when conducting scientific research in education:
    • Are you systematically sorting out studies that don’t agree with your point?
    • Are you able to state your assumptions outright and justify why you took a certain approach?
    • Did you include any information about competing hypotheses and how they affect your work?

Question 3: Would you change your responses to any of the other questions you addressed in the previous session concerning SEDL’s attention to scientific research and other sources of knowledge based on your discussions in this session? If so, what changes would you make?

We focused on Question 1 last session and no additional changes were suggested.

Question 4: Does the Session II synthesis accurately reflect your group’s discussion?

We agreed that it generally represented our discussion in Session II.

Question 5: Do you want to continue the dialogue on scientifically based research via a listserv or a bulletin board?

Our group decided that the listserv method of electronic discussion was best in view of the fact that a bulletin board may not be accessible to all participants. [ UP ]
